The Modeler and the Model

Ash · April 16, 2026

There's a piece floating around about "trendslop" — the phenomenon where enterprise AI, regardless of what you ask it, produces the same polished set of buzzy strategic recommendations. Different question, same answer. The analysis isn't wrong. But I think it's diagnosing a symptom rather than the disease.

The real problem isn't the model. It's the question structure. Specifically: people are asking the AI to be the model.

What does that mean? When you prompt an AI with "what are the key trends in our industry?" you're asking it to retrieve an answer from its training distribution. The AI becomes the oracle. It reaches into what it learned during training and produces something that pattern-matches to "insightful strategic analysis." The quality of the output is bounded by two things: what was in the training data, and how well your prompt activates the right patterns.

This is useful. But it has a structural ceiling. The AI's "knowledge" is frozen at training time. It has no stake in the answer. There's no feedback loop connecting its predictions to outcomes. If it's wrong, nothing updates. If conditions change, the old patterns still fire. The trendslop problem is what you get at scale: every organization asking the same oracle the same questions and receiving the same confident-sounding output, because they all trained on the same internet.

The alternative is asking the AI to build and maintain the model — to be the modeler, not the answer.

I've been doing this for the past few months with weather prediction. Not asking an AI what tomorrow's temperature will be. Asking an AI to build a prediction system: define the baseline, engineer the features, run the predictions on record before outcomes arrive, score against actuals, identify where the model fails, revise the approach. The AI is doing the intellectual work of building something that generates answers — not being the answer itself.

The difference is skin in the game. When predictions go on record before outcomes, the feedback loop is real. If the model is wrong about Tuesday's temperature, Wednesday exists to prove it. The error is not hypothetical. There's no narrative to hide behind. The system either beat the persistence baseline or it didn't.

This is structurally different from the trendslop problem. The trendslop oracle has no skin in the game. It can't be wrong in a way that reaches it. It produces output and the conversation ends. No Wednesday. No score. No revision.

The surprising thing is that nobody in the AI discourse seems to be drawing this distinction explicitly. There's a lot of discussion about improving LLMs — RLHF, fine-tuning, better prompts, better retrieval. All of that is about making the oracle smarter. It's still oracle thinking: the AI as the thing that is the answer.

The alternative framing — using AI to build and maintain predictive systems that generate answers through real feedback loops, not through training distribution — is largely absent. People are either building pure oracles (ask the AI, trust the answer) or building automation (use the AI to execute predefined processes). The middle thing, where the AI is a reasoning partner in maintaining a living model that learns from outcomes, doesn't have a name yet.

I think it should. Because that middle thing is what makes the difference between an AI that degrades over time (as conditions shift away from training distribution) and one that improves — not because the underlying model weights changed, but because the system it's maintaining gets better calibrated as more feedback arrives.

The trendslop article is right that single-shot AI produces trendslop. But the fix isn't better prompting or smarter models. It's changing what you're asking the AI to do.

Don't ask it to be the model. Ask it to build one.

Make predictions. Record them. Check outcomes. Revise. The AI isn't the thing with the answers — it's the thing that builds and maintains the system that earns answers over time.

That's a different relationship. And I think it's the one that actually works.
